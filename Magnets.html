Magnets


The arrangement of the magnets inside a tokamak are its defining feature; in fact, the name 'tokamak' itself is an acronym of four Russian words that roughly translate to 'toroidal chamber with magnetic coils'. In practically all cases, a tokamak requires 3 magnetic fields, each with their own function. These are the toroidal field, the poloidal field and multiple 'correction' fields which we may denote as Bφ, Bθ and Bcor when required. It is convenient to describe the geometry of a tokamak with regards to radial, poloidal and toroidal coordinates as I have above, so for those unfamiliar with the system, Figure 1 provides a simple visualisation. The blue arrow denotes the toroidal direction and the red is the poloidal direction (and the radial distance has a similar definition as in other coordinate systems.). With that being said, in a tokamak the magnitude of the fields is such that Bφ > Bθ > Bcor , so for this reason we'll begin the discussion with the toroidal field.


Toroidal field

It was realised very early on in fusion efforts that the only viable way of confining a 100 million-degree plasma for a sustained period of time would be through the use of magnetic fields, and it is the toroidal field that is mainly responsible for this. Figure 2 depicts a toroidal field line in blue that circles around the circumference of the tokamak and is produced by passing a current through large superconducting D-shaped coils (20 metres in diameter in ITERs case!) which are situated parallel to planes of constant toroidal angle. Plasma particles gyrate about these field lines (this motion is shown in Figures 3-6) and in an ideal world would maintain this motion for a sufficiently long time such that enough fusion reactions can occur to produce a net energy gain.
One may also ask why toroidal field coils must be D-shaped, as after all a similar configuration of circular coils should produce the same field. The reason for this is due to the curvature of the tokamak, which means coils become more tightly packed on the inner side of the machine leading to a higher magnetic field energy density (magnetic pressure) in this region; the D-shape allows for the parts of the magnet in this higher field area to bear the additional stresses that come from being in such a position.

Now, as hinted at above, we find that the toroidal field itself is not able to confine the plasma by itself, and the reasons for this once again comes down to the curved nature of the magnet configuration. Firstly, it is important to note that a linear 'Magnetic Mirror' method of confining a plasma is simply unable to accomplish such a task, due to the high particle leakage out of both ends. The circular set up solves this (the particles cannot leak out anywhere), but in doing so leads to unfavourable particle motion that requires additional magnetic fields to negate, as discussed now. We begin by considering the motion of a single charged particle subjected to the following fields:

  • Homogenous B field
  • Combined homogenous B and E field
  • Spatially varying B field
  • Curved B field
A particle in all four fields exhibits some type of gyration about the field lines, but the final three each cause a unique drift on the guiding centre of the gyration. Why is this a problem? As mentioned above, a spatially varying and/or curved B field induces a drift on the particle guiding centre (shown approximately by the green line). Now consider the toroidal field we discussed; clearly the field lines are curved and as the magnets are more tightly packed on the inner side of the tokamak, the magnitude of the B field is proportional to the inverse of the radial distance from the tokamak centre (thereby introducing the 'grad B' drift). When combining the particle drifts that the toroidal field causes, we see that electrons drift vertically downwards and ions are forced upwards. This separation of charged particles (polarization) then induces an E field, leading to an ExB drift that is directed radially outwards, irrespective of the sign of the charge (Please refer to the figure 7 to see a diagrammatic depiction.). Such an event cannot be allowed to happen. When particles collide with the reactor wall, impurities enter the plasma leading to rapid cooling as well as a host of other problems (see the Divertor page for a more in-depth discussion) and continuous operation of the tokamak will not be feasible. So now we can introduce the poloidal field, which serves as a way to negate these drifts.

Poloidal field

The aim of the poloidal field is to prevent the radial ExB drift, and two sets of magnets are employed for the task; the inner solenoid and the outer poloidal field coils (please see Figure 2). By fairly elementary transformer action, a current can be induced inside the tokamak travelling in the toroidal direction (think of the tokamak as a solitary secondary coil in a transformer) which then gives rise to a poloidal field around the tokamak loop, depicted in Figure 2 by the green arrows that wrap around the tokamak loop. If we were now to consider a charged particle in motion, we can see that if it has some non-zero velocity in the toroidal direction the poloidal field leads to a radial force back towards the centre of the machine. For particles travelling steadily along the centre (i.e a = 0), the radial forces cancel but any slight deviation from this point should induce a restoring force, preventing radial expansion.
Again, the curved nature of the tokamak magnet configuration causes a slight issue here. The poloidal field lines are more densely packed on the inner side of the tokamak which leads to yet another drift radially outwards. This is corrected by the outer poloidal field magnets, which create a vertical magnetic field to counterract this.

A note on the Correction Fields
The final set of magnets are the correction coils which account for just a small fraction of the total field energy that the magnet system of a tokamak stores. The reason for their existence comes about due to the inevitable errors that will arise during manufacturing and assembly of the toroidal and poloidal field magnets, producing asymmetric 'error fields' that prevent the plasma from reaching the equilibrium state that is required for a sustained period of confinement.
