Energy Economics


Once a functioning fusion reactor is developed, one of the next stages is widespread integration onto the electric grid. This is not as simple as hooking it up to a few pylons and pressing the ON button as multiple other factors now come into play, one being the cost of the electricity that the reactor produces. Of course, if the advent of fusion reactors does not provide atleast a small financial incentive to utility companies, it is unlikely that they will be be implemented on the scale that some of us envision. For this reason, the following text will be devoted to analysing the cost of producing electrical power, as well as where fusion will fit into the mix, now and in the future.
As we need to compare the costs of fusion energy with other current sources, this page will be less tokamak-oriented. To start, we'll answer the question of how one goes about quantifying the cost of producing electricity and discuss the variables that this is dependent on. After that, the economics of a tokamak will be looked at; we'll see which components make up the largest proportion of this cost, and what is being/could be done to tackle this. Comparisons will be drawn with other methods of electricity generation, especially nuclear fission due to the many similarities that a fusion and fission power plant will have. Let's get into it.

Calculating Costs

How does one go about putting a price on electricity? The conventional metric used for such a task is the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE). If we consider the total lifetime costs of a power source, the LCOE is the cost at which each unit of energy produced over the lifetime of this source must be sold at in order for the investment to break-even. This is expressed mathematically in Figure 2. The numerator of this fraction is, as expected, the total lifetime costs of our plant, with It representing the loan repayments (with interest) per year, Mt denoting the operation and maintenance costs and Ft the fuel costs, all in year t. An I0 term is also included to account for the initial investment costs.  The denominator is simply the total energy produced over n years of operation. The LCOE is often estimated many years before the end of operation of a power plant, so it is required to make predictions for costs and revenues decades into the future. For this reason, a discount rate (1+r)t is included in the equation, which aims to account for the inflationary nature of money over time (as well as other factors, such as the perceived risk of the investment). In short, a discount rate allows one to consider cashflows in future years in terms of the value of money at the present moment.
But this is not the full story.To further understand the costs of producing electricity, we need to also consider what is known as the capacity factor (C). This is defined as the ratio between the actual electricity output of a source and the maximum possible electricity output over some unit of time.  So for example, if an array of solar panels produced n KWh of electrical energy over say, a week, but it has the potential to produce pn KWh per week, then its capacity factor is p-1. To see why such a quantity influences electricity costs, consider the position of a utility company that is operating a power plant with a 50% capacity factor. From the LCOE, we have seen that the cost of electricity is given by the total cost divided by total energy output,  so by only operating at 50% capacity the plant effectively doubles its cost (by producing only half of the energy that the capital investment endowed it to). In countries such as the UK, this is seen mainly as a problem for intermittent power supplies due to erratic behaviour of the weather on which they are so highly dependent. A very rough but simple quantification of this shows us that plant operating with a capacity factor of Κ will see an increase in the LCOE of a factor Κ-1.

Tokamak Economics


Let's return to nuclear fusion and the tokamak now. This section will look at the various parameters that comprise the LCOE from a fusion perspective as well as what is/could be done to run these costs down in order to make fusion competitive with other baseload power supplies as soon as possible.   
The easiest component of the LCOE to address here is fuel, in which fusion triumphs over practically all other baseload energy sources. It is commonly known that 1kg of fusion fuel (which partly comprises of the abundant hydrogen isotope deuterium, found in any water source on the planet) has the potential to release the equivalent thermal energy of millions of kilograms of coal; such elegance and efficiency in the process of energy extraction is one of the reasons that attracted me, and many others I'm sure, to the fusion industry. Tritium is the other participant in the fusion reaction being targeted here on Earth, so if a self-sustaining tritium breeding blanket is engineered (this is discussed on the reactor wall page) then the fuel costs are almost negligible.
The operation and maintenance costs (denoted by Mt) should not be too difficult to estimate due to the similarities with the baseload power stations today. Even though the fusion reactor is an amalgamation of some of the very greatest feats of engineering seen in history, its primary purpose remains to turn water into steam, like all other thermal power plants.  For this reason, one could gain a ballpark figure of the Mt costs of a proposed fusion plant through comparison with a currently operational thermal power plant of similar output. of which anuclear fission plant seems the most sensible. Of course, there will be a few differences between fission and fusion power plant operations. Whilst fusion does avoid directly producing highly radioactive waste products, the high energy 14.1 MeV neutron produced in D-T (deuterium -tritium) reactions can lead to neutron activationfootnote of surrounding materials, which causes parts of the reactor to become radioactive and reduces its structural integrity. The plasma facing walls of a tokamak may be required to be replaced relatively often due to this high neutron flux (every 6-7 years3), which will contribute to a large chunk of maintenance costs. Note that the activated materials have a radioactive half-life many orders of magnitude less than that of fission products, so fusion power stations will still incur costs of dealing with radioactive materials, but to a much lesser extent than their fission counterparts. The study of materials that are both suitable to use inside a reactor and which are not greatly activated by high neutron fluxes is a topic of very high interest at the moment.
The final component of the numerator of the LCOE is one that accounts for the initial and loan repayment costs of a power plant. This is perhaps the hardest cost to predict as many of the components required by a fusion reactor are unique and have been developed solely for this purpose, so it remains to be seen how low these costs can be driven without first building an operating fusion power plant. We'll draw some comparisons with fission along the way, and see what could be done to avoid the cost overruns that the construction of a nuclear power plant in the present day is infamous for.

These, along with other costs that I have omitted for now (such as maintenance costs) sum to give a direct cost of $6000 million. Indirect costs (overhead and administrative expenses) as well as contingency costs (to make up for uncertainty in the direct costs above) then leave us with the final $8500 million figure. A quick scan of the table above shows that most of the costs are accounted for by fusion-specific components (75% of direct costs in fact). We can expect these 'prototype' components that will be employed in DEMO to see a noticeable fall in price once larger scale production begins.
Finally, we can begin to make comparisons with other electricity sources. The two metrics we use will be the LCOE (defined above) and the TCOE (LCOE modified to include external costsfootnote-external cost defn) although the paper in which the figures have been taken from displays several graphs that provide further insight into the economics of a fusion power plant, which I highly recommend taking a look at.

It was found that the limit sales price of one MWh of fusion generated electricity (using the DEMO model) will roughly be $175 dollars. The current market price at the time of writing was $34 in the EU, thereby incurring a $141 subsidy to make the fusion energy competitive. Again, this appears to be a large amount but compared to the subsidies required by wind and photovoltaic methods of generation it is the lowest figure (subsidies for intermittents must account for having a baseload power supply on standby in the case of insufficient supply). Quantifying the relevant terms listed at the start of this page, the paper then goes onto conclude that the LCOE and TCOE of a first generation fusion power plant will be around $110 and $120 respectively. Where do these figures stand in comparison to other electricity generating sources? Well, of the 7 sources considered (fusion, fission, coal, natural gas, offshore wind, onshore wind and solar), fusion's LCOE places it as 5th cheapest, but its extremely low external costs then rank it second cheapest when considering the TCOE (bested only by current nuclear fission technologies). Considering that this analysis has been undertaken on perhaps what will be the first commercial fusion power plant, the result above is very promising.
It is worth stressing again that these are only very rough figures, as they must be when analysing the economic performance of a power plant that is predicted to only begin operation midway through this century. Costs of electricity from DEMO will be higher than predicted here due to the neglection of interest payments on the loan taken to fund such an endeavour. Such factors cannot be left unaccounted for in the next (and final) section, where we address the shortcomings of nuclear power in the short time that humanity has been able to tame it, as well as what can be learnt from this half a century of experience.
